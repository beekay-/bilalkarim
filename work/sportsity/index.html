Sportsity

Discover, share, and rank nearby courts for your favourite outdoor sports.

Visit Site

Where to play?

When I moved to a different neighbourhood in Calgary, I initially couldn’t find the nearest court to play tennis. Eventually I did find one, but it was always too crowded. I wondered if other nearby courts existed where I could go and play.

Power of open data

I decided to create a responsive web app to help users discover nearby courts for outdoor sports in Calgary. I started working with City of Calgary’s open data catalogue to prepare a database of court locations for outdoor sports.

Find, share, and rank

Besides helping users find the nearest court for their chosen sport such as tennis, soccer, or volleyball, I also wanted to show directions to those locations by using Google Maps on either web, iOS, or Android (depending on what the user had installed on their device). Users could also share the location of the court with their friends using various channels such as SMS, Twitter, or Facebook, and even had the ability to rank courts—by crowdsourcing this information.

Process and involvement

This type of dataset did not exist anywhere else except at a city’s open data catalogue website. Even then, it had to be cleaned and assembled into a proper geospatial format. Once the dataset of court locations various outdoor sports was ready, I started developing the front-end of the application using Google Maps API, while my good friend Rustam Kamberov started working on the back-end in Java.

Challenges

Several challenges were experienced during each phase of the product lifecycle.

Data management. Homeowners vary across multiple age groups—from 25-34 to 65+. How does one create a responsive web application that is universally intuitive and easy-to-understand regardless of the age group? Furthermore, the perception of energy efficiency may also vary from city to city. How does one accomodate for those changes in perception throughout the tone and language of MyHEAT?

Future ideas

Some ideas could not make it to the initial release of this project due to technical and cost limitations, but will be worth exploring in future.

Real-time tracking. One idea early was to use sensors for real-time tracking of court usage. At the time, cost and scale became an issue, but as these sensors become more affordable every day, this feature might be worth trying. It would also be possible to ask users to check into certain courts, but then the user has to remember to check in. Our goal was always to help them find locations to the nearest courts.

Multi-city expansion. Now that the codebase is ready, other cities like Toronto, Vancouver, Edmonton, and more can be added to the map to take Sportsity global. The definite requirement is the availability of open datasets, which may not be an issue. But even if it is, there are other ways to gather data such as classifying satellite images or crowdsourcing locations from users.
